Chocolate Production and Consumption

Background Information

Chocolate is derived from the fruits of cacao trees and as its popularity grew, this led to the development of cacao tree plantations. Cocoa comes from cacao beans and is the highly processed form of cacao e.g., by roasting beans at high temperatures.

Did you known that roughly 400 to 450 cocoa beans are needed to make just one pound (450g) of chocolate? Each cacao tree produces around 2500 beans on average so chocolate production is definitely no easy work.


So where do our cocoa beans come from?

As of 2020, Cote d’Ivoire, Ghana, Ecuador and Nigeria were the top exporters of cocoa beans. Cote d’Ivoire, located in West Africa was leading by a big margin with over 3.6 billion USD worth of cocoa bean exports. Cote d’Ivoire is the leading exporter worldwide producing over 2 billion kilograms of cocoa beans each year. Ghana is the second largest cocoa producing country.


Let’s look at how cocoa production has changed over time

Africa has consistently been the largest producer of cocoa over the last 60 years, accounting for 70% of the world’s cocoa beans.

Since 1985, Africa has seen a steady increase in cocoa production. Asia and South America have seen a small upward trend whilst production has barely increased in Oceania and North America.


Which countries eat the most chocolate around the world?

Switzerland is the top consumer of chocolate with each person consuming nearly 9 kilograms of chocolate per year. European countries take up the top ranks and appear to be biggest chocoholics.

Australians, New Zealanders and Americans also love their chocolate.

On the lower end of the spectrum, countries in Asia, South America and Africa appear to not have as much of a sweet tooth. To compare, a person in Switzerland eats around 88 times more chocolate than someone in China.


How does the location of the chocolate company impact ratings?

Chocolate bars were rated out of 5:

  • 1 to 1.99 correspond to Poor
  • 2 to 2.99 correspond to Below Average
  • 3 to 3.99 correspond to Satisfactory
  • 4 to 5 correspond to Excellent

Chocolate produced in Sao Tome, Switzerland, France, Italy and Belgium have the highest proportion of ratings classified as Excellent. However, the proportion of Poor chocolates in Sao Tome were just as much as the amount of Excellent chocolates. Across the board, it appears that Italy and Switzerland were the most consistent with 89% and 87% respectively of Satisfactory and Excellent classifications in total.


How do cocoa content and ingredient amount impact chocolate ratings?

Cocoa content refers to the % of the chocolate that is made from cocoa beans. Milk chocolate tends to have around 10-50% cocoa whilst dark chocolate has 50-90% cocoa.

It appears that generally, chocolates with cocoa content around 60-80% hit the sweet spot and consistently yield higher ratings, regardless of the number of ingredients. Ratings tend to get lower when the cocoa content leaves this range. Overall, it appears that the number of ingredients in the chocolate do not greatly impact consumer preferences.
